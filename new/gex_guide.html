What is GEX?

GEX (Gamma Exposure) measures the total gamma that options market makersMarket makers are firms/traders that provide liquidity by always being ready to buy or sell. They hedge their risk by trading the underlying stock/index when options prices move. hold across all strike prices. It tells us how much delta-hedgingDelta-hedging: when market makers buy or sell the underlying asset to offset the directional risk of their options positions. This mechanical buying/selling is what creates support and resistance. dealers must do for every $1 move in the underlying asset.

Think of it like this...

Imagine a ball on a curved surface. Positive gamma is like a bowl — the ball always rolls back to center (mean-reversion). Negative gamma is like a hill — any push sends the ball accelerating away (trending).

Chapter 03

HVL — Highest Volume Level

The strike price with the highest options open interest and volume. It represents the point of maximum dealer exposure and acts as a gravitational center for price.

Analogy: The Magnet

HVL is like a powerful magnet placed on a number line. Price (a metal ball) is constantly pulled toward it. The further price drifts, the stronger the pull. Near expiration, the magnetic force is strongest.

Price Above HVL

Bullish Bias

HVL acts as support. Dips toward HVL tend to be bought by dealer hedging. The level below acts as a floor.

Price Below HVL

Bearish Bias

HVL acts as resistance. Rallies toward HVL tend to be sold. The level above acts as a ceiling.

Key Properties of HVL

1

Strongest Near Expiration

As options expiry approaches, gamma increases sharply at the HVL strike, making the "pinning" effect most powerful on expiration day.

2

Dynamic — Shifts Intraday

New options volume can shift the HVL. Large block trades at a different strike can move the magnet mid-session.

3

Works Best in Positive Gamma

When the market is in positive gamma territory, HVL pinning is strong. In negative gamma, the magnet weakens and price can break away violently.

Chapter 04

GEX Flip — The Volatility Regime Switch

The price level where total dealer gamma switches from positive to negative. This is arguably the single most important GEX level because it determines the entire character of the market.

Analogy: The Weather Line

Imagine a line on a map. North of the line: calm, sunny weather (positive gamma). South of the line: storms and hurricanes (negative gamma). The GEX Flip IS that line.

Positive Gamma Regime

◆

Dealer Hedging: Counter-Trend

Market makers sell when price rises, buy when it falls. They act as shock absorbers, dampening moves.

◆

Volatility: Compressed

Daily ranges are tight (0.3-0.5%). VIX stays low. Intraday feels boring and choppy.

◆

Best Strategy: Sell Premium

Fade extremes, sell strangles/iron condors. S/R levels are respected. Mean-reversion wins.

Negative Gamma Regime

◆

Dealer Hedging: Pro-Trend

Market makers buy when price rises, sell when it falls. They amplify every move like fuel on fire.

◆

Volatility: Expanding

Daily ranges blow out (1-3%+). VIX spikes. Fast, directional moves dominate.

◆

Best Strategy: Trade Momentum

Buy breakouts, buy puts for protection, reduce position size. S/R levels break easily.

Chapter 05

Call Wall — The Ceiling

The strike with the largest positive call gamma. It represents the level where upward momentum stalls because dealers aggressively sell the underlying to hedge.

Analogy: The Glass Ceiling

The Call Wall is like an invisible ceiling. You can jump toward it, sometimes tap it, but enormous force pushes you back. Only a massive catalyst can break through. In positive gamma, it's reinforced steel. In negative gamma, it's thin glass.

Chapter 06

Put Wall — The Floor

The strike with the largest positive put gamma. When price falls here, dealers buy the underlying to hedge, creating a bounce.

Analogy: The Trampoline

The Put Wall is like a trampoline. When price falls onto it, dealer hedging pushes it back up. But if price falls with enough force (a panic), it can rip through — and then there's nothing below.

The Call Wall is at 5850. Price approaches and touches 5848 three times but fails to break through. What is happening?

Random coincidence
Retail traders are selling at round numbers
Market makers are selling the underlying to delta-hedge their short call exposure, creating resistance
The Put Wall is pulling price down
At the Call Wall, dealers who sold calls must sell the underlying to stay delta-neutral. This creates mechanical selling pressure that acts as resistance. Each approach triggers more hedging.
Question 3 of 5

Price just broke below the GEX Flip. What should you expect?

Volatility expansion — dealers now amplify moves instead of dampening them
Price will immediately crash to zero
Nothing changes — GEX Flip is just informational
Volatility will decrease because puts are being bought
Below the GEX Flip, dealers switch from counter-trend hedging to pro-trend hedging. They now sell into drops and buy into rallies, amplifying volatility. This is the most important regime change in GEX analysis.
Question 4 of 5

If the Put Wall breaks and price crashes through it, what happens mechanically?

Dealers start buying aggressively, creating a V-shaped bounce
Nothing — the Put Wall was just a suggestion
VIX drops because fear has been resolved
Dealers must sell to re-hedge, creating cascading selling and accelerating the decline
When the Put Wall breaks, dealers who were buying to hedge now need to sell to adjust. This creates a feedback loop of selling — the exact mechanics behind waterfall declines and flash crashes.
Question 5 of 5

Price will go sideways with no directional bias

HVL acts as a gravitational magnet. When price is below it, the pull is upward. However, since price is approaching from below, HVL initially acts as resistance — price wants to get there but may struggle to break above.
